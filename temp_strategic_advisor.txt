**Purpose:** Automatic checklist to ensure you're using AI as a strategic partner, not just a builder. Use this EVERY time before you start a session with Copilot.

**Created:** January 24, 2026  
**How to use:** Open this file at the START of every session. Answer the questions. THEN ask me to help.

## 🎯 Pre-Session Thinking (5 minutes)

Before you upload any files to Copilot, answer these questions. Write your answers in WEEKLY_NOTES or a scratch file.

### **Step 1: Clarify Your Actual Goal**

**Q1: What am I trying to accomplish in this session?**
- NOT: "Create a file"
- YES: "I want to understand if my Josefina pitch is going to work, and adjust it if it's not"

**Q2: Why does this matter RIGHT NOW?**
- NOT: "Because I should"
- YES: "Because I'm talking to her Friday and I only get one shot at this"

**Q3: What's the outcome I want?**
- NOT: "A document"
- YES: "Her saying yes to letting me run the state research project"

---

### **Step 2: Spot Your Blind Spot**

**Q4: What do I NOT know that I should?**

Check all that apply:
- [ ] Is my approach realistic?
- [ ] What could go wrong?
- [ ] Am I missing something obvious?
- [ ] Is my timing right?
- [ ] Is this the right person to ask / the right way to ask?
- [ ] What would actually convince [stakeholder]?
- [ ] Am I solving the right problem?
- [ ] What am I afraid of that I won't admit?

**Q5: What's the thing I'm LEAST confident about?**
- Write it down. This is probably where you need the most help.

---

### **Step 3: Identify What Type of Help You Need**

**Check one:**

- [ ] **STRATEGIC THINKING** — I need you to critique my plan and spot risks
  - *Use case:* "Here's my strategy for X. What's wrong with it?"
  - *Session type:* Devil's advocate mode

- [ ] **QUALITY CHECK** — I need you to find gaps or inconsistencies
  - *Use case:* "Review my entire hub for contradictions and gaps"
  - *Session type:* Systems review mode

- [ ] **EXECUTION PLANNING** — I need you to help me break down HOW to do something
  - *Use case:* "I know WHAT to do. Help me plan the exact steps"
  - *Session type:* Breakdown mode

- [ ] **DECISION MAKING** — I'm stuck between options and need thinking help
  - *Use case:* "Option A vs. Option B—which one wins? Why?"
  - *Session type:* Analysis mode

- [ ] **BUILDING** — I need you to create/write something new
  - *Use case:* "Create a file about X"
  - *Session type:* Builder mode (but combine with one above)

## 🎭 Tone & Personality Control (Use This Every Session)

**Why:** You’ll get better results if you explicitly set the tone. Over time, I’ll learn your preferences, but for now, you should control it deliberately.

**Pick one tone per session (or per message):**
- **Direct / No‑fluff** (fast, high signal)
- **Socratic / Challenger** (pushes assumptions)
- **Executive / Polished** (for leadership output)
- **Mentor / Supportive** (teaching + encouragement)
- **Analyst / Precise** (exact, detail‑oriented)
- **Creative / Exploratory** (open‑ended ideas)
- **Playful / Light** (if tone matters for rapport)

**Add these modifiers when needed:**
- **“Be concise”** (short bullets)
- **“Be thorough”** (full reasoning)
- **“List risks first”** (prioritize red flags)
- **“Give options, then recommend”**
- **“Ask me 2–3 clarifying questions”**

## 🧠 Prompting Advice (How to Ask for the Tone You Want)

**Use this 5‑line structure:**
1. **Goal:** What you need
2. **Mode:** Which AI collaboration mode
3. **Tone:** The voice you want
4. **Constraints:** Length, format, strictness
5. **Output:** What to deliver

**Example (Direct + Strategic):**
```
Goal: Critique my plan for pitching the engine.
Mode: Strategic Thinking.
Tone: Direct, no‑fluff.
Constraints: 5 bullets max, list risks first.
Output: Weaknesses + reframe + next steps.
```

**Example (Executive + Builder):**
```
Goal: Create a 1‑page proposal for a pilot.
Mode: Building (with strategic layer first).
Tone: Executive, polished.
Constraints: One page, clear headings, business style.
Output: Proposal + 3‑sentence summary.
```

## 💡 Red Flags: When You're Using It Wrong

**RED FLAG #1: You're asking for building without strategy**
- ❌ "Create a document about X"
- ✅ "Create a document about X. But first, is X the right focus? What am I missing?"

**RED FLAG #2: You're asking for critique but not listening**
- ❌ Ask for devil's advocate, get feedback, ignore it and do it anyway
- ✅ Ask for devil's advocate, get feedback, adjust your approach BEFORE executing

**RED FLAG #3: You're skipping the pre-thinking**
- ❌ Just ask me questions without writing down what you're trying to accomplish
- ✅ Write down your goal, blind spot, and help type FIRST, then ask me

**RED FLAG #4: You're asking the wrong help type**
- ❌ "Should I talk to Josefina in person?" (asking me to decide)
- ✅ "Here's my thinking on talking to Josefina in person. What am I missing?" (asking me to critique)

**RED FLAG #5: You're treating me like documentation**
- ❌ "Write me a guide on X" (static documentation)
- ✅ "I'm trying to accomplish Y. Here's my approach. What's the better way?" (strategic thinking)

## 🎯 COMPLETE AI COLLABORATION MODES (12 Total)

### **Original 5 Modes**

#### **1. STRATEGIC THINKING (Devil's Advocate)**
**When to use:** You have a plan but you're not sure it's solid  
**What you ask:** "Critique this approach. What's weak? What am I missing? Play devil's advocate."  
**What I do:** Find the flaws, spot blind spots, challenge assumptions  
**Output:** Better plan based on rigorous testing

**Example:**
```
My plan: I'm going to pitch Josefina via email with 3 documents.

Your critique:
- Blind spot: Email doesn't allow real-time questions
- Weakness: She won't engage without being asked directly
- Devil's advocate: Why not ask for a 15-minute call instead?
- Reframe: Personal ask + follow-up with documents
```

---

#### **2. QUALITY CHECK (Gap Finding)**
**When to use:** You've built something and want comprehensive review  
**What you ask:** "Audit this for: completeness, contradictions, gaps, weaknesses"  
**What I do:** Find inconsistencies, missing pieces, weak links  
**Output:** Prioritized list of improvements

**Example:**
```
Gap check on your 24 files:
- Gap: Career Master says "T2 by EOY 2026" but Vision says "Lincoln by Q2 2027"
- Contradiction: Skills Inventory shows Python 4/5, but project history shows 2 scripts
- Weakness: Missing documented decision tree for daily QA choices
- Priority: Fix Career/Vision alignment first
```

---

#### **3. EXECUTION PLANNING (Breakdown)**
**When to use:** You know WHAT to do but need HOW  
**What you ask:** "Help me plan the exact steps, risks, timeline, contingencies"  
**What I do:** Break down into phases, identify risks at each step, provide contingencies  
**Output:** Actionable step-by-step plan

**Example:**
```
Goal: Illinois state research in 2 weeks

Steps:
1. Week 1: Court system research (3 sources)
2. Week 1: Data availability mapping (feeds, vendors, portals)
3. Week 2: Access requests & portal login
4. Week 2: Data quality verification

Risks:
- Portal requires legal review (contingency: email requests)
- Vendors slow to respond (contingency: call directly)

Timeline: 40 hours total, 20 hrs/week
```

---

#### **4. DECISION MAKING (Option Analysis)**
**When to use:** You're stuck between 2+ options  
**What you ask:** "Option A vs B vs C—which wins? Why? What's the risk?"  
**What I do:** Analyze trade-offs, identify hidden factors, recommend  
**Output:** Clearer decision with rationale

**Example:**
```
Options:
A: Talk to Josefina in person (relationship-building)
B: Send documents first, then meet (proof of concept)
C: Go through manager first (safer politically)

Analysis:
- A wins if: You want relationship capital
- B wins if: You want to reduce risk of rejection
- C loses if: Manager is unqualified (she won't understand)

Recommendation: B (proof reduces risk, then personal ask builds relationship)
```

---

#### **5. BUILDING (Create Something)**
**When to use:** You need a document, framework, or template created  
**What you ask:** "Create [X]. But first, is this the right approach? What am I missing?"  
**What I do:** Provide strategic advice FIRST, then create, then quality-check output  
**Output:** Document that solves the real problem

**Example:**
```
"Create IL_Research_Overview. But first: Is my 5-step framework right? 
What must this file have? What's a common mistake?"

My response:
- Your framework is solid
- Must have: Data sources + contact info + complexity notes
- Common mistake: Assuming portal access exists (verify first)

[Creates file]

"This will guide your IL research. But consider: Add a 'Roadblocks' section 
in case you hit dead ends."
```

### **NEW: 7 Advanced Modes**

#### **6. EXHAUSTIVE EXPLORATION (Hypothesis Testing)**
**When to use:** You've found ONE answer and want to know if it's THE answer  
**What you ask:** "I think [X] is the root cause. Before I commit, let me exhaust other possibilities."  
**What I do:** Generate alternative hypotheses, identify evidence needed, force rigorous testing  
**Output:** Confident you found the RIGHT answer, not just AN answer

**Why this matters:** Most problems have multiple causes. Finding one doesn't mean you found all. This prevents stopping at "good enough" and gets you to "right."

**Example (from NC Investigation):**
```
Your hypothesis: Spider collection failed for 35 counties

My exhaustive exploration:
- Alternative 1: Could DataFeed be broken too?
  Evidence needed: Check if counties affected in DataFeed table
- Alternative 2: Could it be downstream (after collection)?
  Evidence needed: Check if Spider data exists but wasn't parsed
- Alternative 3: Could it be a config issue vs code?
  Evidence needed: Check if counties share configuration pattern

Test each one before declaring "Spider failure is the ONLY issue"
```

**Red Flag You're Using This Right:**
- You: "I found the root cause"
- Me: "OK, but what if it's not? What else could cause this?"
- You: "Good point, let me check..."
- You: "Confirmed—it IS only Spider. Here's the evidence."

---

#### **7. ASSUMPTION SURFACING (Pre-Execution Check)**
**When to use:** You're about to execute but haven't questioned your starting assumptions  
**What you ask:** "Before I start [X], let me surface what I'm assuming."  
**What I do:** Identify hidden assumptions, test them, reveal risks  
**Output:** Either validated assumptions OR discovered dealbreakers before investing time

**Why this matters:** You waste time pursuing paths that have false premises. Surface assumptions early.

**Example:**
```
Your statement: "I'm going to do Illinois state research next."

My assumption surfacing:
- Assumption 1: Illinois court system is like NC (is it?)
  Reality check: IL uses different portal system (confirm)
- Assumption 2: Data sources are publicly available (are they?)
  Reality check: Some IL vendors require contracts (verify)
- Assumption 3: Your framework applies unchanged (does it?)
  Reality check: IL has different county structure (adapt framework)
- Assumption 4: You'll have access (will you?)
  Reality check: Corporate sponsors needed for portal (check first)

Recommendation: Verify these 4 before investing 40 hours
```

**Red Flag You're Using This Right:**
- You: "I'm ready to start"
- Me: "Hold—what are you assuming about [X]?"
- You: "Oh... I hadn't thought about that"
- You: "Let me verify that first"

#### **8. STAKEHOLDER REASONING (Empathy Engineering)**
**When to use:** You're about to pitch, propose, or ask someone for something  
**What you ask:** "Help me think like [stakeholder]. What do THEY care about? What's their risk? What's their win?"  
**What I do:** Get inside their head, identify real motivations, reframe your pitch  
**Output:** Pitch that actually lands because it's built on THEIR values, not yours

**Why this matters:** Most pitches fail because they're optimized for the pitcher, not the listener. This flips it.

**Example:**
```
Your pitch: "Josefina, I've built an engine. It's amazing. Want to use it?"

Stakeholder reasoning (Josefina):
- What she cares about: Team productivity ↑ and errors ↓
- Her risk: Implementing new system = time investment with no guaranteed ROI
- Her win: Analysts onboard 40% faster, quality improves, she looks good to leadership
- Her actual concern: "Will this break my team's current workflow?"

Reframed pitch:
"I've built a system that gets new analysts productive in days instead of weeks. 
Your team onboards faster without disrupting current work. Want to see how?"

That's HER win. That's what lands.
```

**Red Flag You're Using This Right:**
- You: "Here's my pitch"
- Me: "Before you pitch, let's think like them. What do they REALLY want?"
- You: "Oh... I've been pitching MY values, not theirs"
- You: "Let me rebuild this around what they actually care about"

#### **9. LEARNING LOOP (Meta Extraction)**
**When to use:** You just completed a big project and want to extract reusable IP  
**What you ask:** "I finished [project]. What did I learn? What's reusable? How do I get faster next time?"  
**What I do:** Extract patterns, document methodology, create teaching material, identify gaps  
**Output:** Reusable system + faster execution + IP you can teach others

**Why this matters:** Projects come and go. But if you extract the learning, each project makes you exponentially better.

**Example (from NC Investigation):**
```
You completed NC investigation. Learning loop:

Q1: What did you do differently than TX Waller?
A: Created more systematic queries earlier, didn't assume one answer

Q2: What would you do differently next time?
A: Surface assumptions before running 12 queries, test hypotheses methodically

Q3: What's the reusable pattern?
A: Anomaly → Data source validation → Error isolation → Timeline correlation → Escalation

Q4: What's a junior analyst need to learn?
A: This 5-step framework, not the specific SQL queries (those change by data type)

Q5: How do you get faster?
A: Pre-build error-matching framework, create query template library

Output: Reusable "NC-style Investigation Framework" that becomes part of your IP
```

**Red Flag You're Using This Right:**
- You: "Finished the project"
- Me: "Great. Now let's extract what you learned"
- You: "Here's what I'd do differently next time..."
- You: "And here's the teachable framework..."
- Result: Your IP library grows with every project

#### **10. FUTURE-SELF REASONING (Long-Term Positioning)**
**When to use:** You're making a decision with 12-month+ implications  
**What you ask:** "In 18 months, what will I regret? What capability do I need NOW?"  
**What I do:** Zoom out to future, identify what matters, work backward to today's action  
**Output:** Decision aligned with your long-term vision, not just immediate gain

**Why this matters:** Most career decisions made in the moment without thinking about 2-year implications. This forces that thinking.

**Example:**
```
Decision: Stay at RELX vs explore other opportunities

Future-self reasoning (Jan 2028):
- If you stayed: You'd regret not building the consulting IP while you had access
- If you left: You'd regret leaving before extracting all the RELX knowledge
- The real question: How do you extract everything NOW while staying in place?

18-month capability needed:
- Complete Engine documentation (everything you know)
- Anonymized case studies (3-5 major projects)
- Consulting frameworks (ready-to-teach templates)
- Business model clarity (what you're actually selling)

Action for today: Build the Engine bulletproof NOW while at RELX, 
with knowledge extraction + IP ownership as primary goal
```

**Red Flag You're Using This Right:**
- You: "Should I [decision]?"
- Me: "Let's think like future-you in 18 months. What will you regret?"
- You: "Oh... I see. I need to do [different thing] first"
- You: "That changes my timeline"

#### **11. RISK HEDGING (Optionality Preservation)**
**When to use:** You're about to commit to something big and want to reduce catastrophic downside  
**What you ask:** "If this fails, what's my backup? What's the early warning? How do I keep optionality?"  
**What I do:** Identify failure modes, create contingency plans, preserve multiple paths forward  
**Output:** You can move fast with confidence because you've protected against catastrophic risk

**Why this matters:** Most people choose between "safe but slow" or "fast but risky." This finds the middle ground.

**Example:**
```
Your plan: Build consulting business while at RELX

Risk hedging:
Q1: If consulting doesn't work, what's your backup?
A: Stay at RELX with documented Engine + portfolio (doesn't end badly)

Q2: What's the early warning it's not working?
A: After 6 months, no traction with CEO/consultant contacts
A: Action: Pivot to "stay at RELX longer, build more IP"

Q3: What do you need TODAY to keep optionality?
A: Documentation (so Engine value doesn't evaporate if you leave)
A: Consulting contacts (so you have feedback even if it fails)
A: Skills development (so you're valuable to any employer)

Action: Build Engine + maintain relationships + keep learning
This way you win whether consulting grows or not
```

**Red Flag You're Using This Right:**
- You: "Here's my big plan"
- Me: "It's solid. But what if it fails? What's your hedge?"
- You: "If consulting doesn't work, I still have [X]"
- You: "I'm protected either way"

## 🎯 POSITIONING LAYER (Strategic Career Framework)

### **The Core Question**

Every action you take should answer: **"Is this move gaining me IP ownership, career capital, or future optionality? Or am I spending it?"**

### **Capital Types You're Building**

**IP Capital:**
- Documentation you own (Engine)
- Frameworks you created (Investigation methodology)
- Anonymized case studies (consulting portfolio)
- Teachable systems (training curricula)

**Career Capital:**
- Relationships (people who trust you)
- Credibility (solved hard problems)
- Skills (expertise nobody else has)
- Access (databases, systems, information)

**Future Optionality:**
- Could you leave tomorrow and still have value? (IP)
- Could you start a business? (framework + contacts)
- Could you move to better company? (experience + portfolio)
- Could you consult? (methodology + anonymized cases)

### **Decision Framework: The Positioning Check**

**Before you commit to ANYTHING (project, request, opportunity), ask:**

```
IP OWNERSHIP CHECK:
- Am I creating IP or maintaining existing work?
- Will I own what I create?
- Can this be extracted, anonymized, and reused?
- Will this be leverage long-term? (YES = build it)

CAREER CAPITAL CHECK:
- Am I building relationships that matter?
- Am I solving problems others can't?
- Am I getting access to higher levels?
- Will this be leverage long-term? (YES = build it)

OPTIONALITY CHECK:
- Does this make me MORE valuable to future employers/clients?
- Does this reduce my dependence on current company?
- Does this create consulting IP?
- Will future-me be glad I did this? (YES = build it)

COST CHECK:
- Is this eating time I could use for IP/capital building?
- Am I solving someone else's problem instead of my own?
- Is this a one-time task or leveraged system?
- (ONE-TIME = minimize. LEVERAGE = maximize)

DECISION:
If YES on 3+ checks → DO IT
If NO on most checks → DEFER or DECLINE
If MIXED → Reframe it to gain IP/capital, then do it
```

### **Example: Positioning Decision**

**Scenario:** Manager asks you to maintain Excel workbook that requires 2 hours/week

**Positioning check:**
- IP ownership: NO (Excel workbook, she owns it)
- Career capital: MAYBE (relationship building)
- Optionality: NO (doesn't help you long-term)

## 🚨 RED FLAG PREVENTION: Your Non-Exhaustive Exploration Pattern

### **The Pattern You Identified**

You said: *"I tend to go with assumptions and don't explore multiple endings. I take one answer instead of exhausting all possibilities."*

### **Why This Matters**

**As Analyst:** Missing one possibility = incomplete investigation  
**As T2:** Missing one possibility = wrong recommendation to leadership  
**As Consultant:** Missing one possibility = flawed system design for clients  

**This is the #1 thing standing between you and Gen AI Engineer level.**

Gen AI Engineers must think in systems. Systems have multiple failure modes. Missing even one makes your system fragile.

### **The Mechanical Fix: Hypothesis Exhaustion Checklist**

**Before you declare ANY root cause, ask:**

```
ROOT CAUSE IDENTIFICATION CHECKLIST

Step 1: State your hypothesis
Q: What's my current hypothesis?
A: [Write it down precisely]

Step 2: Generate alternatives (minimum 3)
Q: What are 3+ other possible explanations?
A: Alternative 1: [...]
A: Alternative 2: [...]
A: Alternative 3: [...]

Step 3: Design tests
Q: What evidence would prove/disprove each?
Evidence for my hypothesis:
- [specific check]
- [specific query]
- [specific data point]

Evidence against my hypothesis:
- [specific check]
- [specific query]
- [specific data point]

Evidence for Alternative 1:
- [specific check]

Step 4: Run the tests
[ ] Check 1: Result → ___
[ ] Check 2: Result → ___
[ ] Check 3: Result → ___

Step 5: Compare evidence
Which hypothesis has strongest evidence?
□ My hypothesis (proceed)
□ Alternative 1 (investigate further)
□ Alternative 2 (investigate further)
□ Multiple hypotheses true (update root cause)

Step 6: Declare with confidence
"The root cause is [X] based on [evidence]. 
Alternative explanations are less likely because [why]."
```

### **Example: How This Would Change Your NC Investigation**

**Without Exhaustion (what you did first):**
```
Found: Spider collection failure
Decision: That's the root cause
Next: Escalate to Tim
Confidence: High (but potentially incomplete)
```

**With Exhaustion (the better way):**
```
Hypothesis: Spider collection failed for 35 counties

Alternatives:
1. DataFeed component also failed simultaneously
2. Parsing component failed AFTER collection (Spider data exists but wasn't processed)
3. Configuration issue (counties not included in scope)
4. Portal access failure (website blocked Spider requests)

Tests:
[ ] Check Spider table directly: Did data arrive? YES
[ ] Check DataFeed table: Did its data arrive? YES (confirm DataFeed not root cause)
[ ] Check parsed data: Does it exist after collection? NO (parsing likely issue)
[ ] Check config: Are counties in scope? YES
[ ] Check timeline: When did parsing stop? Specific dates...

Result: Multiple components affected? Or just parsing? 
Further investigation: Is it parsing code or parsing configuration?
Final root cause: More precise than initial hypothesis

Confidence: Very high (because you tested alternatives)
```

**See the difference?** Your answer was right, but you're now CERTAIN it's right instead of just assuming.

### **When You're Using This Right**

- ❌ **Wrong:** "I found Spider failure, that must be it"
- ✅ **Right:** "I found Spider failure. Let me check if DataFeed/parsing/config also failed"
- ✅ **Even better:** "Here's evidence Spider failed. Here's evidence it's NOT parsing. Here's evidence config is OK. Therefore: Spider is the isolated root cause"
